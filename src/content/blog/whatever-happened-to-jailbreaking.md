---
title: "Whatever Happened to Jailbreaking?"
description: "A brief history of iPhone jailbreaking, the Cydia era, and what it said about control, ownership, and curiosity."
category: "Tech History"
date: "December 24, 2025"
---

![iPhone 3GS top view](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b4/IPhone_3GS_-_top_view-0394.jpg)

Apple has been releasing iPhones for almost twenty years now. The first one came out in 2007 with Steve Jobs saying, “Today, Apple is going to reinvent the phone.” And at the time it really did feel like that.

But what I always forget is how quickly people started trying to break it.

The first iPhone was locked to AT&T. You could not just use any SIM. You could not install whatever you wanted. There was no App Store yet. It was this beautifully designed object that you were not really allowed to touch beyond what Apple permitted.

From a business point of view, that made sense. Exclusive carrier deal. Tight control. Clean ecosystem.

From the point of view of a teenager who just wanted his phone to work, it was infuriating.

I had this iPhone 3GS sitting on my desk. It was slightly beat up. It looked incredible. It did nothing. SIM locked. Just a dead brick that technically powered on. I was not a technical kid. I knew basic HTML. That was it. The idea of hacking a phone felt ridiculous.

Then I started seeing videos about jailbreaking.

![George Hotz](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/21/George_Hotz.jpg)

George Hotz was one of the early names. Seventeen years old. Used screwdrivers, a guitar pick, a soldering iron. That detail always stuck with me. It was not some corporate research lab. It was someone sitting there figuring it out.

And then tools started appearing. Public jailbreak releases. Apple would push an update. The jailbreak community would release another exploit. It became this ongoing back and forth. Every update from Apple felt like a challenge. Every new exploit felt like a small win.

At first jailbreaking was about unlocking carriers. Using any SIM you wanted. Escaping AT&T. But it quickly became something else.

Once you removed software restrictions, the phone changed. You could install tweaks that Apple would never ship. Custom animations. Changing resolution. Swiping to change keyboards. Fake gravity for icons. Even small things felt massive at the time.

I remember the first time I saw Cydia load on the home screen. That orange icon. That was how you knew it worked. It felt like crossing into a different layer of the device.

![Cydia icon](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Cydia_Icon_ios_7.svg)

Jay Freeman built Cydia before Apple even had an official App Store. You added “sources” which were basically links to repositories full of tweaks. It was messy but it worked. By 2009, millions of devices had Cydia installed. That number still surprises me. It was not just a niche thing.

Apple obviously did not love this.

There were security concerns. Jailbreaking opens up vulnerabilities. There was piracy. Installing cracked apps was easy. That undercut the App Store, which quickly became serious revenue for Apple.

But what I think was really happening underneath was tension. Apple wanted control. End to end control. Hardware and software tightly integrated. The jailbreak community wanted flexibility. Ownership. The ability to modify something they bought.

Steve Jobs once called it a cat and mouse game. That feels accurate.

What I find interesting is that Apple itself came from hacking culture. Jobs and Wozniak built blue boxes to exploit the phone network. That origin story makes the whole thing slightly ironic. I always wondered what Jobs actually thought about jailbreaking privately. He never really spoke about it much.

Over time, Apple started shipping features that originally existed as jailbreak tweaks. Multitasking. Widgets. Customization. Carrier freedom. The gap narrowed. The urgency faded.

The early jailbreak figures moved on. George Hotz pivoted to self driving cars. Cydia stopped taking purchases in 2018. By 2020 it felt like the era had quietly ended.

Now in 2025 my iPhone does what I need it to do. If I want heavy customization I would probably just buy an Android. Jailbreaking feels unnecessary.

But I still remember the feeling of getting my 3GS working. I do not remember the exact steps. I remember reading forums. Watching tutorials. Failing. Trying again. Then finally seeing that Cydia icon appear.

It felt absurdly good.

Not because I had hacked something in some dramatic sense. But because it made the system feel less fixed. Less final. It showed me that the boundaries were just decisions. And decisions can be changed.

Jailbreaking may not matter much now. But that instinct to look at a system and wonder how it works, where the limits are, and whether they are really limits, that part never really goes away.

There are always new systems.

And there is always someone trying to unlock them.
